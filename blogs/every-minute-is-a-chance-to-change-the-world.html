Diana Madibekova

Every Minute Is a Chance To Change The World

Diana Madibekova is a Teaching Fellow and PhD Researcher working on a project on international business and global peace since October 2020. She received an Associate Fellow qualification from Higher Education Academy UK in June 2021. She completed the Legal and Compliance Academy at the Credit Suisse Wroclaw as well as she was working for 2 years as a Regional Compliance Officer in Warsaw, Poland. She volunteered for a number of international NGOs such as British Council, American Corner and UNICEF Kazakhstan. What she is most proud of both in her professional and personal life are the relationships she has built and maintained over the years. She believes that our bonds with those around us make our lives more fulfilling and meaningful, therefore it is important to understand how relationships commence, continue, are cultivated and come to an end in a sustainable and healthy way, however it’s equally vital to remember that the most crucial relationship we will ever have is the one with ourselves. Outside her formal functions, Diana is a guest speaker on panels and projects relating to empowerment of girls and women, peace, public good and better society.

She shares some of her work on LinkedIn and Twitter.

University years and compliance experience

In May 2014, I was attending Seoul National University as an international law exchange student, which is the alma mater of the former United Nations Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon. I completed my undergraduate and postgraduate degrees in international law in Kazakhstan and Poland and completed an internship at the Spanish Embassy in Kazakhstan in 2016. As an intern, I was contributing to reports, media monitoring, management of consular documentation, assisting in organizing cultural events, business forums, official visits between two capitals; preparing diplomatic correspondence such as ceremonial communications and note verbale. In 2017, I became a trainee at the International Office of the University of Wroclaw where I was advising on matters relating to visas, permits and health insurance contracts for students. I first got in touch with compliance during my internship in a Swiss bank between 2017 and 2018. It was a completely new world for me as I had no outstanding knowledge about banking and excel spreadsheets, which later I learnt that the world of finance (and compliance too) is not all about equations but also emotions. Therefore, it connects back to my earlier remark about the importance of human relationships and dialogue; it is vital for young people starting their career to cultivate meaningful bonds with their bosses, mentors and peers. My familiarity with the field of compliance got richer while I was working at a firm in Poland as a Regional Compliance Officer covering the Eastern European Markets’ regulatory risk. I was in charge of managing internal and external stakeholders, communications with regulators and government authorities, ensuring that the relevant compliance programs are in place. As part of that job, I used to go on business trips, but since March last year it as all on pause. In May 2020, I read a Financial Times article that published readers’ opinion on daily routines on hold due to Covid and was positively surprised to see my own brief remark about how we all miss a sense of security and safety.

From compliance to academia

In September 2020, I started my PhD on business and peace under the supervision of excellent professors in a country which populated my childhood, England, where my global career aspirations commenced during the summer of 2010 at Haileybury College in Hertford. My current work focuses on understanding how companies contribute to create peaceful societies and thus lessen conflict and tension in countries where they operate. It remains critical for theoretical, practical and policy implications the way companies uphold compliance standards through their operations locally and globally.

My career trajectory was greatly influenced by my teenage years while I was working part-time for different organisations such as the British Council, and American Corner, an umbrella program within the United States Department of State and met several interesting people. I was taking up on volunteering duties in children’s rights department at UNICEF Kazakhstan in 2013 where I could meet with charge d’affaires to foreign ministers and heads of international organizations. In 2014, I got to meet Roberto Azevedo, who was the-then Director General of World Trade Organisation at a distinguished lecture series at Seoul National University, and the-then German Foreign Minister Frank-Walter Steinmeier during his visit to Kazakhstan at the National Library. One of the most recent conferences I attended I could meet the first woman in history who was in the office of the United States Secretary of State Madeleine Albright at University of Warsaw in March 2019. Throughout the years from my early teenagehood and until now, I have learnt about the complex dynamics of human relationships, how emotions tend to manage us and why it is better if we manage them.

My current academic work also examines the concept of peace as an effective emotion management. In my view, peace is not the expectation of being nice all the time. Peace is, regardless of conflict and collision, being able to build or reset a dialogue, despite the differences that drift people apart. It is a skill of learning to reach a goal while not always declaring oneself as a winner.

My hope is that with my academic work and strength to connect with people, I am able to contribute to the betterment of the world where people opt for dialogue instead of division and where we are all seen for who we really are and not just what we come to represent.



What is one piece of advice you would give to your younger self?

Always believe in yourself, even when no one does. Trust that you are on your own individual unique journey.

If you had to choose an alternative career, what would you be doing now?

International lawyer at the International Court of Justice Headquarters (Peace Palace) in The Hague, The Netherlands.

At the end of your career, if you were to sit and reflect, what one hope do you have?

That I made an impact in the world and in people’s lives in a positive way and contributed to the redefinition of the very essence of peace, including extending my voice to discussions about raising our young girls and boys in a happy and healthy environment so that they can thrive at home and at work which ultimately shapes all of us as a better society.
